  Artificial intelligence is transforming how states manage security and
  governance. AI systems now assist in cyber defence, surveillance, border
  monitoring, and intelligence analysis. These technologies increase state
  capacity but also raise concerns about accountability and institutional
  oversight. For India, this transformation is particularly significant because
  the country faces multiple security challenges, including cross-border
  terrorism, cyber threats, territorial disputes, and information warfare.

  Existing research on AI and national security mainly focuses on technological powers such as the United States and China. Much less attention is given to how emerging powers incorporate AI into their governance institutions. In India’s case, the debate often concentrates on technological adoption or innovation policy. However, the institutional consequences of AI integration for national security governance remain insufficiently explored.

  This paper asks: how is artificial intelligence reshaping India’s national security governance, and what institutional safeguards are required to manage AI-driven security systems responsibly?

  The paper argues that India is gradually moving toward algorithmic sovereignty, meaning the ability of the state to control critical data infrastructures and AI capabilities relevant to security governance. This transition strengthens strategic autonomy and improves decision-making capacity. At the same time, it creates governance gaps related to transparency, institutional coordination, and oversight of automated decision systems.

  The study adopts an institutional and policy analysis approach. It examines national policy documents, cybersecurity frameworks, and digital governance initiatives to understand how AI is being incorporated into India’s security architecture.

  The paper concludes that effective AI governance requires stronger regulatory oversight, institutional coordination, and safeguards for civil liberties. Building such mechanisms will be crucial for integrating AI into India’s national security framework while preserving democratic accountability.

# 1. Introduction

Theintegration of artificial intelligence into state security architecturesrepresents one of the most consequential transformations in contemporarygovernance (Scharre, 2018). Across the world,states are deploying machine learning algorithms, predictive analytics, andautonomous systems in domains once reserved for human intelligence andinstitutional deliberation (Allen & Chan, 2017). This technologicaltransformation has simultaneously expanded the operational capacities ofsecurity establishments and introduced new vectors of institutional risk,including algorithmic opacity, accountability deficits in automated decisionsystems, and structural vulnerabilities to adversarial AI interference (Cummings, 2017). The competitivedynamics of what analysts describe as an artificial intelligence contest amongmajor powers have accelerated pressure on states — including rising powers — tointegrate AI into national security architectures at a pace that frequently outstripsregulatory readiness (Horowitz, 2018).

Withinthis shifting global landscape, digital sovereignty — broadly understood as astate's capacity to govern critical data and digital infrastructure — hasemerged as a strategic imperative rather than a merely administrative concern (Bradford, 2020). Farrell and Newman (2019)demonstrated that control over global information networks confers asymmetriccoercive capabilities upon technologically advanced states, a finding thatcarries particular salience for India. As the world's most populous democracyand a major emerging economy, India confronts a complex and multidimensionalsecurity environment characterised by persistent cross-border terrorism, activeterritorial disputes, escalating cyber intrusions attributed to state andnon-state actors, and an expanding landscape of information warfare (Pant, 2018). Managing thesechallenges demands substantial institutional capacity — a capacity that AItechnologies promise to enhance, but also fundamentally alter in itsinstitutional character (Kello, 2017).

Yet thescholarly literature on AI and national security governance remainspredominantly centred on the United States and China (Scharre, 2018; Dafoe, 2018).The institutional dimensions of AI integration in rising powers such as India —how AI reshapes governance architectures, creates coordination challenges, andgenerates accountability gaps — have received comparatively little systematicscholarly attention (Radu, 2021).India's domestic discourse on AI has tended to privilege innovation policy andeconomic applications over the national security governance implications of AIintegration, a gap that represents a substantive deficit in both academicscholarship and policy analysis (Pant, 2018).This paper seeks to address that lacuna through an institutionally groundedempirical analysis.

This paperasks: how is artificial intelligence reshaping India's national securitygovernance, and what institutional safeguards are required to manage AI-drivensecurity systems responsibly? It argues that India is advancing toward whatthis paper conceptualises as *algorithmic sovereignty* — thestrategic capacity of the state to govern critical AI infrastructure andautomated decision systems relevant to national security, in alignment with itsconstitutional and strategic values. This transition enhances India's strategicautonomy while simultaneously generating significant governance deficits intransparency, institutional coordination, and civil liberties protection (Floridi, et al., 2018). The paper proceedsas follows. Section 2 surveys the relevant scholarly literature across fourthematic clusters. Section 3 develops the integrated theoretical framework ofalgorithmic sovereignty. Section 4 describes the methodological approach. Section5 empirically analyses AI's integration across India's national securityarchitecture. Section 6 examines the resulting governance challenges andinstitutional gaps. Section 7 concludes with implications for scholarship andpolicy.

# 2. Literature Review

# 2.1 AI and National Security

Theacademic literature on artificial intelligence and national security hasexpanded substantially since the mid-2010s, driven by the proliferation ofautonomous weapons, machine learning in intelligence analysis, and the growingmilitarisation of digital infrastructure (Allen & Chan, 2017). Scharre's (2018)foundational study demonstrated that the removal of human judgment from lethaldecision-making raises profound questions of accountability and internationalhumanitarian law, establishing autonomous systems as a central problem ofcontemporary security studies. His subsequent analysis of the four competitivebattlegrounds of AI — data, computing power, talent, and institutionalintegration — provides a structural framework for understanding how statescontest AI advantage in the international system (Scharre, 2023). Horowitz (2018) introduced theconcept of adoption capacity to explain why AI's impact on the balance of powerwill be determined primarily by a state's institutional and economic ability tointegrate AI into military and governance processes, rather than by its capacityfor algorithmic innovation alone. Cummings (2017) further demonstratedthat AI's introduction into military command structures generates novelprincipal-agent dilemmas, as automated systems may pursue operationalobjectives in ways that diverge from the intentions of human commanders anderode traditional chains of accountability. These contributions collectivelyestablish the empirical terrain of AI-enabled security governance while leavinginadequately explored the institutional dynamics of rising powers such as India(Radu, 2021).

# 2.2 Algorithmic Governance

A parallelstrand of scholarship examines algorithmic decision-making within governanceand regulatory systems. Yeung (2017) introduced theconcept of "hypernudging" to describe how Big Data analytics systemscontinuously reconfigure individual choice architectures in real time throughalgorithmically determined correlations invisible to the subjects beingregulated, challenging foundational assumptions of administrative law aboutlegibility and contestability. (Zuboff, 2019) theorised the political economy ofsurveillance capitalism, demonstrating that data-extractive systems introduceasymmetric knowledge structures that concentrate epistemic and coercive powerin state and corporate actors at the expense of individual autonomy. Floridi etal. (2018) proposed a normativearchitecture for trustworthy AI governance built around five principles —beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, justice, and explicability — publishedin *Minds and Machines* and operationalised in a subsequentframework in *Science and Engineering Ethics* (Floridi, Cowls, King, & Taddeo, 2019). These contributionsconverge on a central analytical insight: algorithmic systems are not merelytechnical instruments but institutional actors embedded in structures of powerand accountability (Bradford, 2020). When deployed within securitygovernance, they introduce risks of unchallengeable automated decision-makingthat standard legal frameworks are structurally ill-equipped to constrain (Yeung, 2017).

# 2.3 Digital Sovereignty and Technological Power

Theliterature on digital sovereignty and technological power directly informs thispaper's theoretical contribution. Bradford (2020) demonstrated howjurisdictional authority over digital platforms and data flows functions asstructural power, enabling norm-setting beyond territorial borders throughregulatory hegemony — what she terms the "Brussels Effect." Farrelland Newman (2019) extended thisanalysis through their concept of weaponised interdependence, showing thatstates positioned at the hubs of global information networks acquire asymmetriccoercive leverage over those located at the periphery — a dynamic that directlyshapes India's strategic interest in building autonomous AI infrastructure.Dafoe (2018) framed AI governanceas a new domain of international political competition, arguing that stateswhich fail to develop robust governance frameworks risk both internalaccountability failures and external vulnerability to technologically superioradversaries. Bostrom (2014) underscored themagnitude of institutional challenges posed by advanced AI systems whosedecision-making processes remain opaque even to their designers, a concern thatbecomes acute when such systems govern security-relevant operations. Singer andFriedman (2014) established thatdigital interdependence simultaneously creates operational capability andsystemic exposure, making the governance of that interdependence a core concernof national security strategy.

# 2.4 Indian Strategic Technology Policy

Scholarlyattention to Indian strategic technology policy remains comparatively sparserelative to its geopolitical importance. Pant (2018) provided asystematic overview of India's national security architecture, identifyinginstitutional fragmentation — across intelligence agencies, defence ministries,and civil governance bodies — as a persistent structural weakness that AIintegration simultaneously depends upon and may exacerbate. Mohan (2015)situated India's foreign policy evolution within a framework of strategicautonomy, identifying the tension between India's preference for non-alignmentand its growing integration into great-power technology networks as a definingchallenge for Indian statecraft. Radu (2021), in the firstcomprehensive analysis of national AI strategies, found that India's 2018 NITIAayog document was predominantly ethics-oriented rather than rule-based,favouring functional indetermination over binding regulatory architecture — agovernance posture with direct implications for security AI oversight. Kello (2017)contributed conceptual tools for understanding how cyber capabilities disrupttraditional security paradigms, tools directly applicable to India's expandingcyber defence architecture. These contributions identify a clear research gap:the institutional consequences of AI integration for India's national securitygovernance — particularly with respect to algorithmic sovereignty — remaininsufficiently theorised in the existing literature.

# 3. Theoretical Framework

This paperdevelops an integrated conceptual framework centred on algorithmic sovereignty,a concept derived from and extending the digital sovereignty and technologicalpower literatures (Bradford, 2020;Farrell & Newman, 2019). Algorithmic sovereignty is defined here as astate's capacity to independently govern the design, deployment, and oversightof AI systems bearing on national security, in alignment with its ownstrategic, legal, and constitutional values. This definition encompasses threeanalytically distinct and mutually constitutive dimensions.

The firstdimension is infrastructural control — ownership of and authority over thedata, computing hardware, and algorithmic systems underpinning securityapplications (Scharre, 2023). Dependence onforeign AI infrastructure exposes states to coercive leverage and supply-chainmanipulation, vulnerabilities highlighted by Farrell and Newman’s (2019)weaponised interdependence framework. 

The seconddimension is decisional autonomy — the ability of state institutions to makesecurity decisions without structural constraints imposed by foreign platformsor opaque external algorithms (Dafoe, 2018).Scharre (2023) notes this will growcritical as autonomous systems compress decision cycles.

The thirddimension is governance capacity — institutional and regulatory frameworksensuring AI-enabled security systems operate within legal and ethicalconstraints (Floridi et al., 2018; Yeung, 2017). The framework treatsalgorithmic sovereignty as dynamic and multidimensional (Horowitz, 2018). It distinguishes India’s advancinginfrastructural sovereignty from weaker governance sovereignty (Radu, 2021), with implicationsfor democratic AI governance (Floridi et al., 2019; Bradford, 2020).

# 4. Methodology

This paperemploys a qualitative research design grounded in institutional and policyanalysis (Radu, 2021).The institutional analysis approach is appropriate for studying theorganisational, regulatory, and normative dimensions of AI integration withinnational security governance, where quantitative data are either classified,unavailable, or insufficiently meaningful without interpretivecontextualisation (Pant, 2018).A qualitative design also permits systematic engagement with primary governmentdocuments and secondary scholarly literature, enabling the criticalinterpretive synthesis that AI governance research in this domain requires (Dafoe, 2018).

Primarysources consulted include: NITI Aayog's (2018) National Strategyfor Artificial Intelligence, which established India's foundational AIgovernance approach; the Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023);the India AI Governance Guidelines (2025) issued by theMinistry of Electronics and Information Technology; the National Cyber SecurityStrategy (2020) of the National Security Council Secretariat; the Department ofDefence Production's ADITI Scheme documentation (2023); DRDO's Evaluating TrustworthyAI (ETAI) framework; the Ministry of Defence's Technology Perspective andCapability Roadmap 2025; the Principal Scientific Adviser's (2025) report ondemocratising access to AI infrastructure; and CERT-In's Digital Threat Report2024.

Secondarysources include peer-reviewed journal articles from International Security,Minds and Machines, Information, Communication and Society, Policy and Society,and Science and Engineering Ethics; books published by Oxford University Press,Yale University Press, and W.W. Norton; and research outputs from the Centrefor Internet and Society and the Belfer Center. The analysis maps AIintegration across security domains and governance gaps.

# 5. AI and India's National Security Architecture

# 5.1 Cybersecurity Governance

India'scybersecurity architecture has undergone substantial AI-driven transformationsince the adoption of the National Cyber Security Policy in 2013 and itssuccessor National Cyber Security Strategy in 2020 (National Security Council Secretariat, Government of India, 2020). The National CyberSecurity Strategy 2020 established a whole-of-government approach to cyberresilience, mandating the integration of AI-based threat detection andautomated response systems across critical information infrastructure at thenational level (National Security Council Secretariat, Government of India, 2020). The Indian ComputerEmergency Response Team (CERT-In) has deployed AI-driven Security Informationand Event Management platforms enabling real-time correlation of networkanomaly data across millions of endpoints, improving the speed and accuracy ofintrusion detection compared to manual analysis protocols (CERT-In; CSIRT-Fin; SISA, 2025). CERT-In's DigitalThreat Report 2024, produced in collaboration with CSIRT-Fin and SISA,identified AI-powered anomaly detection, behavioural analytics, and dark webmonitoring as priority defence mechanisms for India's critical sectors,reflecting governmental recognition that adversarial AI necessitates defensiveAI at equivalent scale (CERT-In; CSIRT-Fin; SISA, 2025).

The IndiaAI Governance Guidelines (2025), released under theaegis of the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology, represent themost significant formalisation of India's AI governance architecture to date (Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology, Government of India, 2025). These guidelinesare anchored in seven governing principles — termed "sutras" — andestablish a coordinated institutional leadership structure comprising the AIGovernance Group, the Technology and Policy Expert Committee, and the AI SafetyInstitute (Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology, Government of India, 2025).By situating AI oversight within a whole-of-government framework with MeitY asnodal ministry, the guidelines demonstrate convergence between innovationgovernance and national security requirements that Radu (2021) identifies as amarker of AI governance maturity. The Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023)reinforces this architecture by regulating personal data processing andfiduciary obligations, creating a statutory foundation for AI accountability indata-intensive security applications (Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology, Government of India, 2023).These developments reflect what Bradford (2020) terms the strategicconstruction of a domestic regulatory ecosystem — a form of softinfrastructural power that reduces exposure to external normative frameworksand strengthens the governance dimension of algorithmic sovereignty.

# 5.2 Surveillance and Intelligence Systems

India'sAI-enabled surveillance infrastructure has expanded markedly in scale andoperational sophistication, driven by the imperatives of counter-terrorism andborder security (Centre for Internet and Society, 2023). The NationalIntelligence Grid (NATGRID), conceptualised following the 2008 Mumbai attacksand operationalised progressively, integrates over twenty critical databasesspanning immigration, banking, telecommunications, and identity records,providing authorised agencies with a near-real-time operational intelligencepicture (Centre for Internet and Society, 2023). NATGRID'sarchitecture exemplifies the infrastructural dimension of algorithmicsovereignty, consolidating previously fragmented data holdings into a unifiedanalytical platform under centralised state authority (Pant, 2018).The rollout of *Gandiva*, NATGRID's AI-ML-enabled analyticscomponent, marks a qualitative transformation in India's intelligencearchitecture by enabling entity resolution, pattern matching, and associativeinference across heterogeneous datasets at population scale (Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology, Government of India, 2025).

Thesesurveillance developments raise substantive questions about the relationshipbetween security capacity and democratic governance. The integration ofbiometric identity data from the Aadhaar ecosystem with NATGRID's intelligencedatabases creates a comprehensive population surveillance architecture that,while operationally powerful, operates in a legal framework developed prior tothe AI era (Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology, Government of India, 2023).Yeung's (2017) analysis ofhypernudging is directly pertinent: algorithmic profiling systems do not merelyidentify threats but actively shape the categorical frameworks through whichpopulations are assessed, rendering the internal logic of surveillanceself-perpetuating and resistant to challenge through conventionaladministrative law mechanisms. Similarly demonstrated that data-extractionsystems tend toward expanding their scope and epistemic reach over time unlessbounded by robust institutional constraints, a dynamic that India's evolvingsurveillance architecture illustrates with particular clarity. The SupremeCourt of India's affirmation of the right to privacy as a fundamentalconstitutional right in *K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India* (2017)establishes a constitutional benchmark against which the proportionality ofAI-enabled surveillance must ultimately be evaluated (Rajkumar & Singh, 2023). These tensionsbetween security capacity and constitutional rights underscore the importanceof governance capacity as the third dimension of algorithmic sovereignty (Floridi, et al., 2018).

# 5.3 Defence and Strategic Applications

India'sdefence establishment has pursued a systematic programme of AI integrationunder the strategic logic of reducing dependency on foreign platforms andbuilding indigenous capability — a direct operationalisation of the decisionalautonomy dimension of algorithmic sovereignty (Ministry of Defence, Government of India, 2025). DRDO's DirectorGeneral articulated this strategic logic explicitly in February 2026, assertingthat in the defence domain, India "cannot afford to depend on solutionsand AI models which are coming from abroad" and must ensure the "trustworthinessof the models and the systems which we are adopting" (Economic Times, 2026). This institutionalposition reflects a principled commitment to algorithmic sovereignty'sdecisional autonomy dimension — the insistence that AI systems governing lethaland strategic operations must be domestically controlled to ensureauditability, integrity, and accountability (Cummings, 2017). The DRDO has developed the EvaluatingTrustworthy Artificial Intelligence (ETAI) framework to provide technical andinstitutional standards for building resilience into AI-enabled defencesystems, complemented by validation and verification guidelines governing AIsolution development across the defence production cycle (Economic Times, 2026).

The ADITI(Acing Development of Innovative Technologies with iDEX) Scheme, operatingunder the Department of Defence Production, targets approximately thirtydeep-tech critical and strategic technologies — including AI systems, quantumcomputing, advanced cyber capabilities, and autonomous unmanned vehicles — foraccelerated development with the explicit objective of reducing dependence onforeign original equipment manufacturers (Department of Defence Production, Ministry of Defence, Government of India, 2023).The Ministry of Defence's Technology Perspective and Capability Roadmap (TPCR)2025 identifies AI-enabled command and control systems, autonomous unmannedaerial vehicles, and predictive maintenance platforms as priority developmentareas for the Indian armed forces (Ministry of Defence, Government of India, 2025). These initiativesrepresent precisely what Horowitz (2018) characterises asadoption capacity-building: the deliberate institutional investment required toensure that AI capabilities can be meaningfully integrated into operationalmilitary contexts, rather than remaining isolated technical demonstrations thatfail to achieve strategic relevance. The IndiaAI Mission, approved by the UnionCabinet in 2024 and establishing a national public compute infrastructure ofover 34,000 GPU units, demonstrates that India's defence AI strategy issupported by a sovereign computing infrastructure commensurate with operationalambitions (IndiaAI, 2024).

# 5.4 Digital Infrastructure and Data Governance

India'sDigital Public Infrastructure (DPI) stack — comprising Aadhaar biometricidentity, the Unified Payments Interface, DigiLocker, and the AccountAggregator framework — constitutes the foundational data architecture uponwhich AI-enabled governance systems, including those with securityapplications, are progressively built (Principal Scientific Adviser to the Government of India, 2025). The National DataGovernance Framework (2023) establishes protocols for data access,interoperability, and standardisation across government datasets, creating thestructural preconditions for large-scale AI deployment across both civiliangovernance and security domains (Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology, Government of India, 2023).The Principal Scientific Adviser's (2025) report on democratising access to AIinfrastructure identified the integration of AI with Digital PublicInfrastructure as one of three foundational enablers for India's AI governancestrategy, reflecting a whole-of-government recognition that DPI and AIgovernance are structurally interdependent (Principal Scientific Adviser to the Government of India, 2025).

India'sapproach to digital infrastructure governance reflects a strategic logicconsistent with Bradford's (2020) analysis ofregulatory ecosystem construction: building a large, interoperable domesticdigital architecture creates structural leverage and reduces dependency onforeign platform architectures that Farrell and Newman (2019) identify as aprincipal source of geopolitical vulnerability. The IndiaAI Missioninstitutionalises this logic at the national level, explicitly framing AIcapability-building as simultaneously an economic competitiveness imperativeand a matter of strategic autonomy (IndiaAI, 2024). The principle-based, techno-legalapproach embedded in the India AI Governance Guidelines (2025) — combininglegal accountability frameworks with technical interventions delivered throughpremier research institutions — illustrates India's evolving model ofalgorithmically sovereign governance, one that seeks to internalise thenormative, institutional, and technical dimensions of AI governance within acoherent national framework (Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology, Government of India, 2025).The alignment between India's DPI stack, its national AI computeinfrastructure, and its emerging AI governance architecture positions India asan increasingly significant actor in the global debate on how rising powers canpursue both AI capability and governance accountability simultaneously (Radu,2021; Bradford, 2020).

# 6. Governance Challenges and Institutional Gaps

DespiteIndia's significant advances in AI-enabled security governance, the transitiontoward algorithmic sovereignty generates substantial institutional andnormative tensions that demand critical and analytically rigorous examination.The first and most fundamental challenge concerns algorithmic opacity —the structural resistance of AI decision-making systems to external scrutiny (Yeung, 2017). AI systems deployedin surveillance, population-scale profiling, and cyber defence operate throughdecisional processes whose internal logic is not readily accessible to affectedindividuals, oversight bodies, or courts, fundamentally challenging the administrativelaw principle that decisions affecting rights must be made legible andchallengeable (Floridi, et al., 2018). The epistemologicalopacity of systems such as Gandiva, which performs entity resolution andassociative inference across heterogeneous national databases, generatesoperational intelligence without producing auditable decision trails adequatefor judicial or parliamentary oversight (Centre for Internet and Society, 2023).

Second, the accountability of automatedsystems remains institutionally underdeveloped relative to the operationaldeployment of AI in security contexts (Radu, 2021).India's current legislative framework — principally the Information TechnologyAct (2000) and the Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023) — was notdesigned with AI-specific accountability mechanisms in mind, and appliesconcepts of data fiduciary responsibility that were developed for commercialrather than security contexts (Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023).While the India AI Governance Guidelines (2025) introduce a risk classificationapproach and mandate human oversight for high-risk AI applications, theirprinciple-based and non-binding architecture may be structurally insufficientto constrain security applications where operational exigencies routinelycreate pressure to bypass procedural constraints (Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology, Government of India, 2025).Floridi et al. (2018) identify this gap —between normative principle and institutional effectiveness — as a genericweakness of national AI governance frameworks that India must urgently address.

Third, AI-enabled surveillanceinfrastructure poses serious and well-documented risks to civil liberties (Rajkumar & Singh, 2023). NATGRID'sintegration of biometric identity data with intelligence databases raisesproportionality questions under the right to privacy affirmed by the SupremeCourt in Puttaswamy (2017), particularly giventhe absence of judicial authorisation requirements for database queries and thelack of independent oversight of automated profiling decisions (Centre for Internet and Society, 2023). Zuboff's (2019)analysis of surveillance capitalism is structurally pertinent: data extractionsystems tend toward epistemic expansion and scope creep unless bounded byrobust institutional constraints that India's current legal framework does notyet provide.

Fourth, the institutional coordinationdeficit within India's fragmented national security establishment creates AIgovernance risks that are distinct from and potentially more significant thanany individual capability gap (Pant, 2018).The AI Governance Group, the AI Safety Institute, and the sectoral regulatorybodies established under the 2025 guidelines represent important institutionalinnovations, but the horizontal coordination mechanisms between these newbodies, existing intelligence agencies, DRDO, and the Ministry of Defenceremain underdeveloped, creating risks of regulatory arbitrage andaccountability gaps at institutional interfaces (Dafoe, 2018).Addressing these governance deficits will require not merely new regulatoryinstruments but sustained and politically demanding institutional reform ofIndia's security governance architecture (Kello, 2017).

# 7. Conclusion

This paperhas examined the emergence of algorithmic sovereignty as an organising conceptfor understanding India's AI-enabled national security governance transition.It argued that India is pursuing a deliberate strategy of building indigenousAI capability and data governance infrastructure across four operationalsecurity domains — cybersecurity, intelligence, defence, and digital publicinfrastructure — that constitutes a measurable advance in what this paperdefines as algorithmic sovereignty (Horowitz, 2018; Scharre, 2023). Theempirical evidence reviewed demonstrates that India has achieved significantmomentum in the infrastructural dimension of algorithmic sovereignty, throughthe IndiaAI Mission's national compute infrastructure, DRDO's ETAI framework,the ADITI Scheme, NATGRID's AI-enabled intelligence architecture, and the IndiaAI Governance Guidelines (2025) (Ministry of Electronics and InformationTechnology, 2025; IndiaAI, 2024; Department of Defence Production, 2023).

Thepaper's theoretical contribution lies in its multidimensional conceptualisationof algorithmic sovereignty, distinguishing between infrastructural control,decisional autonomy, and governance capacity as analytically distinct butmutually constitutive dimensions of the concept (Bradford, 2020; Dafoe, 2018).This framework reveals that India's progress is structurally asymmetric:advances in AI capability and data infrastructure have not been matched bycommensurate development in regulatory oversight, institutional coordination,or civil liberties protection — generating a governance deficit that representsboth a democratic accountability challenge and a strategic vulnerability(Floridi et al., 2018; Zuboff, 2019). As AI becomes a structurally definingfeature of security governance globally, the choices made by rising powers likeIndia will shape the normative architecture of international AI governance(Dafoe, 2018; Farrell & Newman, 2019).

India'sdemocratic credentials and its aspiration to serve as a responsible AIgovernance leader create both a normative obligation and a geopoliticalopportunity to develop security AI frameworks that reconcile strategiccapability with constitutional accountability (Radu, 2021; Bradford, 2020).Achieving this balance will require statutory oversight mechanisms for securityAI, parliamentary scrutiny of automated surveillance systems, independent auditcapacity consistent with *Puttaswamy* (2017), and principledmultilateral engagement on autonomous systems and algorithmic governance norms(Scharre, 2018; Kello, 2017). Building algorithmic sovereignty in its fullestsense — integrating governance capacity alongside strategic capability — is nota constraint on India's security ambitions but the foundational preconditionfor their democratic legitimacy and international credibility (Pant, 2018;Floridi et al., 2019).
